Understanding Gluten Intolerance

Gluten is a structural protein naturally found in wheat, barley, and rye. It gives dough its elasticity and helps baked goods maintain their shape. However, for some individuals, gluten can trigger digestive discomfort or immune-related reactions.

Reactions to gluten exist on a spectrum. The two most commonly discussed conditions are Celiac Disease and Non-Celiac Gluten Sensitivity (NCGS).

Celiac Disease

Celiac disease is an autoimmune disorder in which the body mistakenly attacks the lining of the small intestine when gluten is consumed. This immune response damages tiny finger-like structures called villi, which are responsible for nutrient absorption.

Over time, untreated celiac disease can lead to nutrient deficiencies, anemia, fatigue, and long-term health complications.

Non-Celiac Gluten Sensitivity

Individuals with non-celiac gluten sensitivity experience symptoms such as bloating, abdominal discomfort, headaches, and fatigue after consuming gluten — but without measurable intestinal damage.

Although the exact biological mechanism is still being researched, evidence suggests inflammation and digestive enzyme limitations may contribute to symptoms.

Why This Matters

Understanding the difference between these conditions is essential when exploring alternative approaches to gluten digestion. While celiac disease requires strict medical dietary management, individuals with mild sensitivity may benefit from supportive digestive strategies.
