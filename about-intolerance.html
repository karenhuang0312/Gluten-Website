Understanding Gluten Intolerance

Gluten is a structural protein naturally found in wheat, barley, and rye. It provides elasticity to dough and structure to baked goods. However, in certain individuals, gluten consumption can trigger digestive or immune-related responses.

Reactions to gluten exist along a biological spectrum, ranging from autoimmune disorders to non-autoimmune sensitivities.

Celiac Disease

Celiac disease is an autoimmune disorder in which the immune system attacks the lining of the small intestine after gluten exposure. This damages the villi — microscopic projections responsible for nutrient absorption.

Long-term damage may result in anemia, nutrient deficiencies, chronic fatigue, and gastrointestinal complications.

Non-Celiac Gluten Sensitivity (NCGS)

Individuals with NCGS experience symptoms such as bloating, headaches, and abdominal discomfort after consuming gluten, without measurable intestinal damage.

Current research suggests inflammation or limited digestive enzyme efficiency may contribute to these reactions.

Why This Matters: Understanding these distinctions is essential when investigating plant-based enzymatic extracts as potential digestive supports. While celiac disease requires strict medical management, individuals with mild sensitivity may benefit from targeted enzymatic assistance.
