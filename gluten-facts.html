Gluten & Celiac Disease: Scientific Overview

🌍 Global Prevalence

Celiac disease affects roughly 1% of the global population.
Many individuals remain undiagnosed due to mild or atypical symptoms. Rates are highest in Europe and North America but are rising globally.

🧬 Genetic Requirement

Most patients carry HLA-DQ2 or HLA-DQ8 genes.
Around 30–40% of people have these genes, but only a small percentage develop celiac disease, showing that environmental triggers matter.

⚠ Autoimmune Response

Gluten triggers immune destruction of intestinal villi.
This damages nutrient absorption and may lead to anemia, bone density loss, vitamin deficiencies, and chronic inflammation.

🥖 Gluten Structure

Gluten is composed of gliadin and glutenin proteins.
Gliadin peptides resist complete digestion, increasing the likelihood of immune activation in genetically susceptible individuals.

🧠 Systemic Symptoms

Symptoms extend beyond the digestive tract.
Neurological effects, fatigue, headaches, joint pain, and skin conditions such as dermatitis herpetiformis are documented.

🔬 Enzyme Research

Scientists are studying protein-digesting enzymes.
Bromelain and papain show potential gluten-breaking properties in laboratory studies, though they are not approved medical treatments.
